# You really do not understand Governance Risk and Compliance until someone shows you how GRC works in life decisions.

![GRC starts to make sense when someone shows you how it works in real life.](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E12AQFf9789yutbNA/article-cover_image-shrink_720_1280/B4EZ4CIPwQHYAQ-/0/1778152181923?e=2147483647&t=eTN-FwfjwV8_z_sJxaP21NQ-5IYTs_VBBAzzDB5IuiU&v=beta)

Governance Risk and Compliance frameworks are important.

Audit checklists are important.

Assessments are important.

None of that replaces learning from someone who has actually been a Chief Information Security Officer and carried responsibility as a CISO. That is where things start to make sense. You learn that risk is not something you put in a table. An audit finding is not something you find during an audit. A control gap is not a technical problem.

Communication with management is not about showing them how much you know about GRC. It is about making the situation clear enough that the right decision can be made about Governance Risk and Compliance.

Over the few Month I have seen how fast you can grow professionally when a good CISO takes the time to explain the thinking behind the Governance Risk and Compliance work. From one week to the next you notice the difference in your work. You become better at doing assessments of Governance Risk and Compliance. More structured in the way you do audits of GRC. More confident in the decisions you make about GRC More precise in the way you communicate about it.

Better at connecting the security work you do to the risk it poses to the business. That kind of development is hard to get from taking courses about GRC.

Courses give you knowledge about Governance Risk and Compliance. Good mentors give you the judgement you need to make decisions about Governance Risk and Compliance.

In the field of cybersecurity and GRC judgement is what separates what you learn in theory from the real value it has in actual operations.

People who invest their time in others to teach them about GRC are incredibly important for the cybersecurity profession and, for GRC.

Thank you.
